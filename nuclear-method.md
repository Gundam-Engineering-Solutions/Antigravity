Given the power supply---specifically the "spider crab" arrangement and tritium polarization---we can proceed with the theoretical calculation for lift.

To achieve "anti-gravity" via electrostatic repulsion, your ship needs to generate an upward force that equals or exceeds the downward force of gravity. Here is the calculation for the specific charge required to hover a $1,000 \text{ kg}$ vessel.

### 1\. The Downward Force (Gravity)

First, we calculate the force the ship exerts downward due to its mass.

$$F_g = m \times g$$

-   **Mass ($m$):** $1,000 \text{ kg}$

-   **Gravity ($g$):** $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

-   **Total Downward Force:** $9,800 \text{ Newtons}$

### 2\. The Upward Force (Electrostatic Repulsion)

To hover, the electrostatic repulsion ($F_e$) must equal the gravitational force ($9,800 \text{ N}$).

$$F_e = q \times E$$

-   **Earth's Electric Field ($E$):** The Earth's surface maintains a net negative charge, creating a downward-pointing electric field. In fair weather, this field strength is approximately $100 \text{ Newtons per Coulomb (N/C)}$ (or Volts per meter).

-   **Charge Required ($q$):** This is the variable we need to find.

### 3\. Solving for Charge ($q$)

Rearranging the formula to solve for $q$:

$$q = \frac{F_e}{E}$$

$$q = \frac{9,800 \text{ N}}{100 \text{ N/C}}$$

$$q = 98 \text{ Coulombs}$$

### The Result

To lift $1,000 \text{ kg}$, your ship needs to maintain a continuous static charge of **$98$ Coulombs**.

Since Earth's surface is negative, your ship must also maintain a **negative charge** of this magnitude to trigger the "like-charge repulsion" described in your research.
